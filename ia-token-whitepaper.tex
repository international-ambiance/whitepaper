\subsubsection{Quadratic Voting: Preventing Whale Domination}

We use quadratic voting to ensure whales can't control everything:

\begin{equation}
\text{Voting Power} = \sqrt{\text{Token Balance} \times \text{Participation Multiplier}}
\end{equation}

This means:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Someone with 100K tokens has 10x the voting power of someone with 1K tokens (not 100x)
    \item Active participants get a multiplier boost (max 2x) for consistent voting
    \item New members have meaningful voice from day one
    \item You can't buy your way to total control
\end{itemize}

\section{Technical Architecture: How This Actually Works}

\subsection{Blockchain Infrastructure}

\subsubsection{Base Layer 2}

IA-Token is deployed on Base, Coinbase's Ethereum Layer 2 network:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Base (L2)}: Primary token contract, governance, and all operations

Why Base:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Extremely low transaction costs (pennies instead of dollars)

\subsubsection{Proposal Lifecycle}

For US readers wondering about securities law, here's how IA differs from a security under the Howey Test:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

    \item \textbf{Expectation of profit}: \textbf{NO}—explicitly disclaimed throughout this document
    \item \textbf{Derived from efforts of others}: \textbf{NO}—value comes from community participation and events, not team efforts to increase value
\end{enumerate}

We believe IA is not a security. But we're not your lawyers, and laws vary by jurisdiction.
